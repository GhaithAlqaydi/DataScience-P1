Introduction

Every year, developers vote with their answers.

Stack Overflow's annual developer survey is the closest thing the tech world has to a census. In 2025, nearly 50,000 software professionals answered questions about everything from their favourite programming language to how they feel about AI taking their jobs.

We analysed those answers using machine learning — training a model to predict job satisfaction from everything the survey captures: experience, pay, remote work, AI tool usage, and more. Here is what we found.

Finding 01

Fear of AI is the single strongest drag on satisfaction.

Of all the factors we measured, one stood out as the clearest negative predictor of job satisfaction: believing that AI will take your job. Developers who answered "Yes" to this question scored meaningfully lower than those who said "No" — more than the effect of any individual pay bracket.

This isn't about AI itself being bad. It's about anxiety. Developers who embrace AI tools report slightly higher satisfaction than the survey average. It's the fear — not the technology — that hurts.

Finding 02

Pay matters — but less than everyone assumes.

Compensation has a positive relationship with satisfaction, but its correlation (r = +0.08) is weaker than experience, and far weaker than the negative drag of AI anxiety. The median satisfied developer earns $75,000 a year. The least satisfied group is not the lowest paid — it's distributed across income levels.

This aligns with decades of psychology research: beyond a comfort threshold, more money produces diminishing returns on day-to-day happiness. What keeps a developer engaged is harder to put on an offer letter.

Finding 03

Experience is quietly one of the best predictors of happiness.

Work experience and years of coding are the two strongest positive correlates in the dataset. The longer a developer has been working, the happier they tend to be — and this holds even after controlling for salary.

The most likely explanation: experienced developers have usually found a domain they enjoy, a company that fits their style, and enough confidence in their skills to feel in control. They are not just wealthier — they are better matched.

In 2025, mindset and flexibility matter as much as the number at the bottom of an offer letter.

Conclusion

The happiest developers aren't necessarily the richest — they're the least afraid.

After analysing nearly 50,000 developer responses, a clear picture emerges. Satisfaction in 2025 is driven by a combination of earned confidence (experience), fair compensation, flexible work, and — above all — a healthy relationship with the AI tools that are reshaping every job in tech.

The developers thriving most are not the ones ignoring AI. They are the ones who picked it up, made it part of their workflow, and stopped worrying about being replaced. The data suggests the best thing any developer — or employer — can do in 2025 is invest not just in tools, but in the confidence to use them.

The code for happiness, it turns out, is mostly about attitude.
